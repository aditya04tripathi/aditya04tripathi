Formal threat modeling can collapse under its own weight: whiteboards full of arrows, six stakeholders, and a Confluence page nobody updates. Small teams still need structured thinking, especially around money, PII, and OAuth tokens.

STRIDE categories give you a checklist without requiring enterprise tooling.

## STRIDE in one sentence each

- **Spoofing:** Is someone pretending to be another user or service?
- **Tampering:** Can data be altered in transit, at rest, or in the UI?
- **Repudiation:** Can a user deny an action you cannot audit?
- **Information disclosure:** Who can see secrets, balances, or support notes?
- **Denial of service:** Can an attacker exhaust your wallet, API, or database?
- **Elevation of privilege:** Can a normal user become an admin or access another tenant?

## Scope the first session to one journey

Pick **one** critical flow: sign-in, link bank account, transfer funds, export statements. Draw four boxes:

1. Client app
2. Your API
3. Data stores
4. Third parties (OAuth, push, email)

For each arrow, ask STRIDE questions. Timebox to ninety minutes.

## Spoofing and session theft

Consumer apps lose sessions to phishing, malware, and device theft. Mitigations stack:

- short-lived access tokens with refresh rotation
- device binding signals where legal
- step-up for high-risk actions

Document what you **do not** solve (rooted devices) so support scripts stay honest.

## Tampering: client is hostile

Never trust the client for balances or entitlements. Signed payloads from the client are still suggestions. All enforcement happens server-side with idempotent commands.

## Repudiation: immutable intent logs

For money movement, store:

- who initiated
- from which session/device fingerprint bucket
- timestamp with server authority
- idempotency key

If disputes arise, you need a narrative, not just a final balance row.

## Information disclosure: minimize blast radius

Separate **authentication identifiers** from **support identifiers**. Mask account numbers in logs. Redact search indexes that do not need raw PAN fragments, better, never store what you do not need.

## Denial of service: economics

An attacker can bleed you via:

- expensive PDF generation
- unbounded export endpoints
- password reset email floods

Per-user quotas, CAPTCHA on abuse-prone routes, and queue-backed heavy work are boring, and effective.

## Elevation of privilege: IDOR hunting

The classic bug: `GET /transactions/123` works when `123` belongs to someone else. Automated tests should include **cross-tenant negatives**. Admin routes need explicit role checks, not “security by URL obscurity”.

## Outputs that survive the meeting

End each session with:

1. A ranked backlog of mitigations (P0–P2).
2. Explicit **accepted risks** with owners and review dates.
3. One diagram committed to the repo under `docs/threat-models/`.

## Cadence

- **Quarterly** full pass on new surface area.
- **Per epic** mini STRIDE when touching auth, payments, or PII schemas.

## Final word

STRIDE is not about perfect safety; it is about **making assumptions visible**. Small teams win by shipping fast **and** writing down what could burn them, then fixing the top of the list relentlessly.
